OpenAI Leadership

Pioneering the future of artificial intelligence for the benefit of all humanity.

A Visionary at the Helm

Sam Altman is a co-founder of OpenAI, established in 2015, and has served as its CEO since 2019. His leadership has been instrumental in shaping OpenAI into a leading force in artificial intelligence research and development. Altman has provided visionary direction for the company, securing crucial funding, including a significant $1 billion investment from Microsoft. He has also fostered key partnerships that have enabled OpenAI to accelerate its research and development efforts.

"We're trying to build AI that is an extension of human will and that is, in the best case, amplifying human will."

Key Contributions

  • Co-founding and Leadership: Altman co-founded OpenAI in 2015 with the mission of developing AI for the benefit of humanity. He has served as the CEO of OpenAI since 2019.
  • Securing Funding and Partnerships: Altman played a crucial role in securing substantial funding for OpenAI, including a $1 billion investment from Microsoft in 2019.
  • Development of GPT Models: Altman has spearheaded the development of the GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) series, including models like GPT-3 and GPT-4.
  • ChatGPT and Public Engagement: Altman guided OpenAI through the launch of ChatGPT, which redefined interactions with technology and set a new standard in natural language processing.
  • Advocacy for AI Policy and Regulation: Altman has been a prominent advocate for AI policy and regulation, engaging with policymakers and governments to shape the regulatory landscape for AI.

OpenAI Timeline

2015

OpenAI is founded as a non-profit research company.

2019

Sam Altman becomes CEO. OpenAI transitions to a "capped-profit" model and receives a $1B investment from Microsoft.

2020

Release of GPT-3, a major leap in language model capabilities.

2022

Launch of ChatGPT, which becomes a global phenomenon.

2023

Release of GPT-4. Altman is briefly ousted and then reinstated as CEO.
